Architecture of the Central Processing Unit

The central processing unit (CPU) is the primary component of a computer that acts as its "control center." A CPU, also known as a central processor, main processor or just processor, is the electronic circuitry that executes instructions comprising a computer program.

1. Core Components

Modern CPUs are incredibly complex. At the most basic level, a CPU is composed of a control unit, an arithmetic logic unit (ALU), and various registers. The ALU is responsible for logical and mathematical operations, while the control unit directs the flow of data.

2. Cache Hierarchy

To minimize the time it takes to access data from the main memory (RAM), CPUs use layers of high-speed memory called cache. Level 1 (L1) cache is the fastest and smallest, typically integrated directly into the CPU core.

3. Instruction Cycle

The operation of a CPU can be broken down into a series of steps known as the fetch-decode-execute cycle. First, the CPU fetches the instruction from memory. Then, it decodes the instruction to determine what action is required. Finally, it executes the operation.
